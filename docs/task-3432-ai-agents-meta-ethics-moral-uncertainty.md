# Meta-Ethics, Moral Uncertainty & Ethical Uncertainty

## Introduction

When an AI system makes decisions that affect humans, whose ethics should it follow? Meta-ethics — the study of the nature of moral judgments — provides a surprisingly practical framework for thinking about how autonomous systems should navigate moral uncertainty.

## What Is Meta-Ethics?

Meta-ethics asks foundational questions: Are moral truths objective and discoverable, or are they invented by humans? Is "murder is wrong" a fact like "water is H₂O," or a cultural preference like "football is better than cricket"?

This question has massive implications for AI alignment and governance.

### Three Major Meta-Ethical Positions

**1. Moral Realism** — Moral truths exist independently of human belief. "Murder is wrong" is true whether anyone believes it or not. If correct, AI can discover moral facts through reasoning, observation, and dialogue.

**2. Anti-Realism / Error Theory (e.g., J.L. Mackie)** — There are no moral facts; all moral talk is systematically mistaken. This doesn't mean ethics doesn't matter — it just means morality is a human invention with real consequences.

**3. Constructivism (e.g., Christine Korsgaard, Sharon Street)** — Moral truths are constructed through rational procedures or reflective equilibrium, not discovered as external facts. This is increasingly influential in contemporary philosophy.

## Moral Uncertainty in Practice

When we are uncertain about which moral theory is correct, how should we act? William MacAskill and others have developed **Moral Uncertainty Theory**:

> *When you're uncertain between multiple moral theories, you should choose the action that maximizes expected moral value, weighting each theory's verdict by your credence in that theory.*

This has practical implications for AI alignment:
- An AI uncertain between utilitarian and rights-based frameworks should hedge
- It shouldn't commit fully to any single ethical theory until evidence warrants it
- The AI should be *transparent* about its uncertainty

## Ethical Uncertainty vs. Moral Uncertainty

These are subtly different:

- **Moral uncertainty**: Which *theory* of ethics is correct?
- **Ethical uncertainty**: How does the correct theory apply to this *specific case*?

A self-driving car faces ethical uncertainty: even if we knew utilitarianism was correct, how should it weigh passenger vs. pedestrian safety in a split-second scenario?

## The Trolley Problem Family & AI

The famous trolley problem has evolved from philosophical thought experiment to engineering constraint. Modern variants include:

- **The trolley problem (classic)**: Switch the track?
- **The footbridge case**: Push someone in front of the trolley?
- **The tunnel case**: What about sacrificing one to save many with less direct action?
- **The trolleybot**: How should an autonomous vehicle program its priorities?

## Tools & Frameworks

| Framework | Application to AI |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Reflective Equilibrium | Align AI values by iterative moral reasoning |
| Expected Moral Value | Choose actions weighted by theory confidence |
| Precautionary Principle | In deep uncertainty, prefer inaction over harm |
| Rawlsian Veil of Ignorance | Design AI systems as if behind a "veil" not knowing your position |

## Challenges

1. **The N+1 problem**: There's always another edge case an AI hasn't seen
2. **Moral disagreement**: Humans can't agree on ethics — how can we expect AI to?
3. **Value alignment**: Whose values should an AI prioritize?
4. **The complexity ceiling**: Full ethical reasoning may require more compute than is practical

## Future Directions

Approaches like **Democratic AI** (using voting mechanisms to aggregate moral preferences) and ** Constitutional AI** (training AI on principles rather than examples) represent emerging strategies for navigating moral uncertainty in practice.
