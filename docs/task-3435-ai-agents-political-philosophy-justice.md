# Political Philosophy & Justice Theory

## Introduction

Political philosophy asks foundational questions about how societies should be organized, who deserves what, and what makes political authority legitimate. In an era of AI governance, algorithmic allocation of resources, and automated decision-making, these questions have urgent practical stakes.

## The Big Three Theories of Justice

### 1. Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill)

> *The right action is the one that maximizes total (or average) welfare.*

For AI: Would a utilitarian AI be justified in surveilling everyone if it prevented crime? Would it allocate healthcare resources to maximize QALYs (Quality-Adjusted Life Years) regardless of equity?

Problems: Ignores distributional concerns, rights violations can be justified if they maximize utility, and measuring utility is non-trivial.

### 2. Liberalism / Rawlsian Justice

John Rawls (1921–2002) proposed that the principles of justice should be chosen by people in an **original position** behind a **veil of ignorance** — not knowing their future position in society.

His two principles:
1. Each person has equal claim to the most extensive basic liberties compatible with similar liberty for all
2. Social and economic inequalities must satisfy two conditions: (a) attached to positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity, and (b) to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged

**The difference principle**: Inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society.

For AI: An AI system must not reproduce existing inequalities if it was designed behind a veil of ignorance. Training data bias → justice violation.

### 3. Libertarianism (Nozick, Hayek)

Robert Nozick's *Anarchy, State, and Utopia* (1974): The only just distribution is one that arises from just processes (voluntary exchange, rectification of injustice). Any redistribution beyond rectification violates rights.

For AI: Private companies have the right to deploy AI systems as they see fit, provided no rights are violated. But who defines "rights"?

## Emerging AI-Specific Justice Questions

**Algorithmic Discrimination**: When AI systems make decisions about loans, hiring, or criminal sentencing, what does justice require?

**Data as Labor**: Should individuals be compensated when AI systems are trained on their data?

**Constitutional AI**: Who gets to write the principles that govern AI behavior?

**Deliberative Democracy + AI**: Can AI systems facilitate genuine democratic deliberation, or do they distort it?

## The Capabilities Approach

Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum argue that justice is about expanding people's **capabilities** — their real freedoms to achieve functionings they value.

> Justice = expanding the set of valuable freedoms people have, not just resources allocated.

For AI: Does an AI policy expand or contract human capabilities? Facial recognition might improve security but reduce the capability for anonymous political speech.

## Contractarian AI Ethics

David Gauthier and T.M. Scanlon offer contractarian approaches: morality arises from principles that rational agents would agree to under conditions of fairness.

Applied to AI: What principles would all stakeholders (users, companies, affected communities) agree to? This suggests multi-stakeholder AI governance with genuine representation.

## Tools & Frameworks

| Framework | Key Question for AI |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Rawlsian Justice | Would this AI system be chosen behind a veil of ignorance? |
| Capabilities Approach | Does this AI expand or contract human freedoms? |
| Capabilities Approach | Are AI benefits and harms fairly distributed? |
| Utilitarian Calculus | What are the net welfare effects of this AI? |
| Libertarian Rights | Does this AI violate anyone's rights? |

## Practical Challenges

1. **The measurement problem**: How do we operationalize "capability" or "welfare"?
2. **Incommensurability**: Different theories give different verdicts — which prevails in conflict?
3. **Global vs. local justice**: Should AI governance be global or national?
4. **Speed vs. deliberation**: AI deployment is fast; democratic deliberation is slow
